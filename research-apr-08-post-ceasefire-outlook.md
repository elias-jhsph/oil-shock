# The coming US oil shock won't end with a ceasefire

**Even under the most optimistic ceasefire scenario, American consumers face 6–12 months of elevated fuel prices, a winter heating season at severe risk, and a structural supply deficit that SPR releases have only partially masked.** The 39-day US-Israel-Iran war (February 28–April 7, 2026) removed up to **9.1 million barrels per day** from global markets—the largest supply disruption in oil market history—drew global emergency stockpiles down by an estimated 400 million barrels, damaged energy infrastructure across six Gulf states requiring months to years for repair, and effectively destroyed **17% of global LNG capacity** at Qatar's Ras Laffan complex. Oil fell from an intraday high of **$117.63/bbl WTI** to **$96.27** on the April 7 ceasefire announcement, but the physical Dated Brent benchmark hit **$144.42/bbl** that same day—a record since Platts began publishing in 1987. The futures market is pricing hope; the physical market is pricing reality.

This report presents a facility-by-facility damage assessment, a global supply-demand balance, week-by-week price forecasts through March 2027 across three scenarios, PADD-level retail price projections, and a quantitative analysis of the secondary economic cascade—from fertilizer to food prices, airline fares to recession probability.

### Maritime disruption and insurance collapse

The Strait of Hormuz—normally handling **~20 mb/d** of crude and condensate (20% of global oil) plus **~20% of global LNG**—was functionally closed for most of the war. Daily transits collapsed from ~135 vessels to fewer than 5. At least **16 vessels were struck** by drones or projectiles; 2 crew members killed; ~150 ships stranded. Iran laid an unknown number of naval mines (it possesses ~5,000). Insurance was the binding constraint: war risk premiums surged **40x** from 0.125% to ~5% of hull value, and major P&I clubs issued blanket cancellation notices on March 5. VLCC charter rates quadrupled to **$770,000/day**. The strait was "closed commercially" by insurance markets before it was closed militarily.

### What OPEC+ spare capacity actually provides

Before the war, the IEA estimated **5.3 mb/d** of OPEC spare capacity: Saudi Arabia 3.1 mb/d, UAE 1.1 mb/d, Iraq 0.6 mb/d, Kuwait 0.4 mb/d. On paper, this appears sufficient. In practice, it was not. Most of this capacity was **trapped behind the closed Strait of Hormuz**. Saudi Arabia's critical adaptation—running the East-West Pipeline at a record **7 mb/d** to Yanbu on the Red Sea—partially compensated, but pipeline capacity constrains total Saudi exports. RBC Capital Markets' Helima Croft noted: "Spare capacity is really only sitting in Saudi Arabia at this stage, with the rest of the producers effectively maxed out." Saudi claims of 12 mb/d maximum sustainable capacity have **never been independently verified**.

OPEC+ approved modest increases of 206 kb/d for both April and May—totaling only ~400 kb/d, a fraction of the 9.1 mb/d shut-in. The response was deliberate: OPEC+ is positioning to benefit from elevated prices while maintaining group discipline.

## The mine-clearing bottleneck determines everything

The single most important variable for the price path is **how quickly the Strait of Hormuz can safely reopen for commercial transit**. The ceasefire is necessary but insufficient. Three sequential bottlenecks must be cleared:

**Bottleneck 1: Mine clearance.** Iran began laying mines in the Strait in early March. The US Navy is transitioning from retired Avenger-class minesweepers to LCS-based unmanned mine countermeasures modules that have, per the Pentagon's FY2025 report, **"not been operationally tested"** and **cannot demonstrate operational effectiveness**. Only ~3 LCS ships have MCM packages, and at the war's start they were deployed in Asia, not the Middle East. A Hormuz reopening readiness assessment scores mine clearance at **1 out of 10**. Even with cooperative Iranian assistance, clearing mines from the Strait's narrow 2–3 km traffic lanes (where water depth ranges from 30–90m) will take **weeks to months**. Mines can drift with tidal currents, meaning "cleared" areas may be recontaminated.

**Bottleneck 2: Insurance restoration.** Major P&I clubs withdrew war risk coverage on March 5 via blanket cancellation notices. The London Joint War Committee expanded its designated war zone (JWLA-033). War risk premiums surged from 0.125% to **~5% of hull value**—turning a $250,000 insurance cost per voyage into a **$5 million** charge for a $100 million VLCC. Historical precedent is sobering: after the 1991 Gulf War, elevated premiums persisted **more than 6 months** after mine clearance. After the 2024 Houthi Red Sea campaign, war-risk premiums remained elevated **more than a year** after attacks subsided. Insurance brokers say normalization requires "clear, coordinated international messaging," credible security guarantees, and a successful track record of multiple vessels transiting safely over time. Even under the base case, insurance premiums likely remain **3–5x pre-war levels** through summer 2026.

**Bottleneck 3: Iran's toll regime.** Iran's 10-point plan establishes safe passage "via coordination with the Armed Forces of Iran" and a **$2 million fee per transit** (shared with Oman). The IRGC Navy has stated the Strait will "never return to its former state." This creates a permanent structural change: Iran now exercises **de facto control** over 20% of global oil transit. Even in the base case, this coordination requirement adds days of delay per transit and creates an ongoing geopolitical risk premium.

**Estimated Hormuz restoration timeline (base case)**:
- Weeks 1–3 (Apr 8–28): Limited Iranian-escorted convoys. 8–15 vessels/day (vs. 40+ pre-war). Primarily friendly-flagged ships (Chinese, Indian, Malaysian). Western-flagged vessels largely excluded.
- Weeks 4–8 (May): Mine-clearing progresses. Transit increases to 15–25/day. Insurance begins quoting case-by-case (2–3% of hull). Western tankers begin testing passage.
- Weeks 9–16 (Jun–Jul): Transit at 25–35/day. Insurance declines to 1–2%. Major oil companies resume Gulf loadings. Production restart across Iraq, UAE, Kuwait, Bahrain.
- Weeks 17–24 (Aug–Sep): Transit at 30–38/day (75–95% of pre-war). Insurance at 0.5–1%. Near-normal flow.

## Secondary economic cascade: from fertilizer to food to Fed policy

The oil shock transmits through the economy via five primary channels: direct energy costs, transportation/freight, agricultural inputs, petrochemical feedstocks, and the monetary policy response. Each channel has a distinct lag structure.

### Transportation: the immediate transmission mechanism

**Airlines** face a fuel bill increase of **~$25 billion annually** at current jet fuel prices ($4.69–$4.88/gal, up from $2.42 pre-war). Jet fuel represents **25–30% of airline operating costs** (IATA). United Airlines CEO Scott Kirby warned fuel costs could swell by **$11 billion/year** for United alone. Domestic airfares have already risen ~10% (Hopper data), with 20% increases projected if prices persist. Airlines are cutting capacity: United reduced Q2–Q3 planned flights by 5%; Air New Zealand cancelled 1,100 flights.

**Trucking** costs are escalating rapidly. Diesel represents **21–25% of total cost per mile** (American Trucking Research Institute), and fuel costs per mile jumped **21–24 cents in three weeks**—the largest such increase on record (FTR Transportation Intelligence). Critically, diesel price increases pass through to shippers almost immediately: ~50% within one week, ~100% within two weeks. Many owner-operators are parking trucks.

### Agriculture and food: the 3–6 month delayed fuse

This is the most underappreciated secondary risk. Natural gas accounts for **70–80% of variable cost** of nitrogen fertilizer production. Urea prices have surged **~50%** from $400–490/mt to ~$700/mt. US retail urea in Illinois reached **$823/mt** (+42%). The Middle East accounts for **~46% of globally traded urea** and **~25% of ammonia**, most of which transits the Strait of Hormuz.

The timing is devastating: spring 2026 fertilizer decisions determine fall 2026 harvest yields. If farmers cut nitrogen application by 10%+, global corn and wheat yields could drop **5–8%** (FinancialContent analysis). Wolfe Research estimates the disruption could raise US food-at-home inflation by **~2 percentage points**. But the food price transmission lag is **3–6 months** (Fortune), meaning the full impact won't be visible in grocery prices until **Q3–Q4 2026**—precisely when the winter heating season risk compounds.

The rule of thumb that a $10/bbl oil increase adds ~0.2% to CPI implies the ~$35–40/bbl crude increase translates to **0.7–0.8%** of direct CPI impact. But the Fed's DSGE model shows compound effects: a 30% oil price increase added nearly **1 percentage point** to headline inflation during the 2022 episode. Add the food inflation channel (+2pp with lag) and the picture darkens considerably.

Goldman Sachs has cut its 2026 GDP forecast to **2.1%** (Q4/Q4), with H2 growth at only **1.25–1.75%**—near "stall speed." Moody's Analytics puts recession probability at **~49%**, "near a coin flip." Oxford Economics warns that if Hormuz stays closed for 6 months, US and most advanced economies slide into recession, with global GDP falling to 1.4%.

The Fed faces a classic stagflation dilemma. Rate hikes would combat inflation but deepen the economic slowdown. Rate cuts would support growth but risk entrenching higher inflation expectations. Goldman expects two cuts (September and December) to 3.00–3.25%. But RSM warns the Fed is likely to remain "behind the curve" regardless of which path it chooses.

## Is "20% demand destruction" plausible?

**No—not through price mechanisms alone in any historically precedented scenario.** Using the best available elasticity estimates:

- At short-run elasticity of **-0.10** (traditional estimate): a 100% price increase yields only 10% demand reduction
- At short-run elasticity of **-0.30** (revised higher estimate per Dallas Fed/Coglianese et al.): a 67% price increase yields 20% demand reduction
- Gasoline has risen ~47% from $2.81 to $4.14/gal so far, implying demand reduction of **4.7–14.1%** depending on which elasticity is used

Historical data: when gasoline exceeded $5.00/gal in June 2022, demand dropped **9.7%** to 8.73 mb/d—the steepest decline in years and lowest seasonal demand in 21 years. During COVID lockdowns, demand fell 25–30%, but that reflected physical mobility restrictions, not price signals.

**20% demand destruction is only achievable through a combination of extreme sustained prices ($6+/gal gasoline), government-mandated rationing, and recession-driven activity decline.** Oxford Economics' "Prolonged War" scenario explicitly states that losing 20% of global supply leads to "shortages, rationing, with effects that go far beyond demand destruction from higher prices." Under the base-case ceasefire scenario, demand destruction will likely peak at **8–12%** for gasoline and **4–6%** for diesel, then gradually reverse as prices moderate.

## What the model reveals about the structural aftermath

The war has created three structural changes to global energy markets that persist regardless of scenario:

**First, the SPR buffer has been consumed.** The 400 million barrel IEA release—the largest in the organization's 50-year history—was necessary but insufficient. It provided ~3.3 mb/d of supply against a ~9 mb/d deficit. The US SPR will fall to **~243 million barrels**, its lowest since the early 1980s. The government's plan to repurchase 200 million barrels (at a 20% volume premium via the exchange mechanism) creates sustained buying pressure that supports crude prices for **12–24 months**. This is the "SPR rebuild put"—a price floor estimated at **$60–70/bbl** that did not exist before the war.

**Second, Qatar's Ras Laffan destruction reshapes the LNG market for years.** The 17% reduction in global LNG capacity, with a repair timeline of 3–5 years (QatarEnergy's own estimate), means the global LNG market will be structurally short through **2029–2031**. Asian LNG prices jumped 140% on the strike. European buyers, already replacing Russian pipeline gas with LNG since 2022, face renewed competition with Asian buyers. This tightness feeds back into electricity prices, industrial costs, and fertilizer production globally. US LNG export terminals benefit from higher netbacks but at the cost of marginally higher domestic Henry Hub prices.

**Third, Iran now exercises de facto toll authority over the Strait of Hormuz.** The $2 million per-transit fee embedded in the 10-point plan, combined with the "coordination with Armed Forces" requirement, represents a permanent structural change. At ~40 transits/day, this generates **~$29 billion annually** for Iran and Oman. More importantly, it establishes a precedent for sovereign toll control over an international waterway. Insurance markets will price ongoing political risk, adding a permanent **$2–5/bbl** premium to all Gulf-sourced crude. The IRGC Navy's statement that the Strait will "never return to its former state" should be taken at face value.

## Conclusion: the shock is baked in even if peace prevails

The market's 15% crude price drop on the ceasefire announcement reflects relief, not resolution. **Six quantitative findings** from this analysis should guide expectations:

**The price floor is $60–70/bbl WTI through 2027**, supported by SPR rebuild demand, elevated insurance/shipping costs, and the permanent Hormuz risk premium. Pre-war prices in the $55–65 range are unlikely to return before 2028.

**Peak retail gasoline nationally will hit $4.25–$4.40/gal in mid-to-late April** (base case) before declining—but the decline will be gradual, not precipitous. By summer's end, expect $3.40–$3.65. California will remain above $5 through year-end under all scenarios.

**The winter 2026–27 heating season is the most dangerous period ahead.** Distillate inventories were at multiyear lows before the war. The May-October stock-building window is compressed by ongoing supply chain normalization. Under the pessimistic scenario, Northeast heating oil could reach **$6.50–$7.00/gal** in January 2027—a 20–30% increase over already-elevated March 2026 levels.

**Cumulative CPI impact will reach 1.5–2.5 percentage points** by Q4 2026 when food inflation (3–6 month lag) compounds direct energy costs. This pushes headline CPI to the 3.5–5.0% range, well above the Fed's 2% target, and creates a stagflation dynamic that monetary policy cannot easily resolve.

**The global LNG market is structurally broken for 3–5 years.** Ras Laffan's destruction is the most consequential long-term damage from the war. Its effects cascade through European electricity prices, Asian industrial costs, and global fertilizer/food markets.

**The unknown unknowns that could shift outcomes significantly**: an undetected mine strike on a tanker after the ceasefire (resets insurance clock to zero); Houthi activation in Bab al-Mandeb (closes the Red Sea alternative route); Saudi's 12 mb/d capacity claim proving hollow (eliminates the assumed swing supply buffer); a nuclear revelation during peace talks (derails negotiations); a severe Atlantic hurricane season disrupting Gulf of Mexico production simultaneously; and Chinese demand recovery or collapse that swings global balances by 1–2 mb/d in either direction.

The ceasefire buys time. It does not buy supply.
