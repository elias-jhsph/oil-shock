## TL;DR
- **Base case (40%): Brent averages ~$100 in Q2, $93 in Q3, $88 in Q4 2026, easing to $80 by Q1 2027** as Hormuz partially reopens (≈8–12 mb/d by July, ≈14–16 mb/d by Q4) but mine-clearing, insurance, and Qatar LNG keep a $15–25 risk premium on the curve through 2027. National retail gasoline peaks at ≈$4.30/gal in May (rolling lag from the April crude print), holds $4.05–$4.15 through July, fades to $3.55–$3.75 by Q4, and California stays >$5.85 (≈$6.20–$6.50 in summer) on top of the $1.21/gal Benicia/Wilmington structural hit.
- **The dominant downside risk is path-dependent: JPMorgan's "operational minimum" window May 9–30 is when storage in Asia hits hard floors, and a single kinetic incident (the Spruance precedent makes this a live tail) takes the curve to $130–150 within days.** A 45% pessimistic case sees Brent average ≈$130 Q2/Q3 and recession beginning Q3 2026 with full-year 2026 GDP at 0.6%, headline CPI peaking near 6% YoY in October, and Fed paralysis through year-end.
- **The under-priced channels are the second-order ones**: (a) Qatar Ras Laffan trains 4 & 6 mean structural LNG loss for 3–5 years and a permanent +$2–3/MMBtu premium on TTF that bleeds through to US Henry Hub by Q3; (b) the spring 2026 urea/anhydrous shock (urea +49% YoY, anhydrous +43% YoY) does not show up in CPI Food-at-Home until October 2026 and peaks in Q1 2027 at ≈+5.5–6.5% YoY; (c) freight rate pass-through to core goods CPI lags ~3–6 months and starts appearing in the July CPI print.

### 8. Second-Order Cascade — Lag Structure & Monthly CPI

### Why the US Shale Lever Is Effectively Dead in 2026
- Baker Hughes oil rig count 411 (April 3) — 70 below same-time 2025; total US 543–548. Frac spread count falling.
- EIA forecasts US production averaging ~13.4 mb/d in 2026 — flat to slightly down. Permian breakeven: $61–$64/bbl (Dallas Fed). Even at Brent $100 sustained, capital discipline + Tier 2/3 inventory degradation + service inflation means lead time to add 500 kbpd is 6–12 months minimum (JPMorgan: an additional 1 mb/d takes 6–12 months).
- DPA invoked April 20 for petroleum is symbolic — it cannot conjure rigs, sand, frac crews, or labor in the timeframe that matters.
- **Net shale response in this model**: +0 to +50 kbpd by Q1 2027 vs. April baseline. We do not credit the US production surge that some optimistic scenarios assume.

### OPEC+ May 3 Meeting — Assumed Outcome
- Confirmed track from April 5: +206 kb/d nominal increase implemented May (Saudi-led "Great Eight"). The meeting communiqué will likely repeat this for June.
- Reality on the ground: Hormuz constraint makes nominal additions academic for Iraq, Kuwait, UAE; Saudi can deliver via Petroline (already at 7 mb/d capacity) and Yanbu (5 mb/d crude exports, 700–900 kbpd products).
- **UAE quitting OPEC** (announced effective end of April 28 week): this is a structural shift that doesn't bite supply in the next 3–6 months but signals the post-war OPEC+ will be smaller and more market-share-oriented. UAE's marginal output discipline was already weakest among Gulf producers; the formal exit accelerates production toward ~3.6 mb/d by Q1 2027 (vs. 3.4 quota).
- Base case assumption: Saudi maintains Petroline at 7 mb/d throughout horizon; offers an additional 500 kbpd from spare capacity if Brent breaches $130; refuses to formally cut prior to Hormuz reopening.

### Demand Destruction & Elasticity Arithmetic
Using Coglianese/Davis/Kilian/Stock (2017) and Dallas Fed updated panel: short-run gasoline price elasticity ≈ –0.31 to –0.37, stable since 2016 (not declining toward zero as older Hughes/Knittel/Sperling 2008 suggested).

- 21.2% MoM gasoline jump (March CPI) → expected ~6.5–7.8% reduction in gasoline consumption within 1–3 months. EIA weekly product supplied data already showing 4-week average down 169 kbpd through April 17.
- Pass-through threshold modeling: At national average $4.50, demand destruction ≈ –4% YoY; at $5.00, ≈ –7%; at $5.50, ≈ –10%; at $6.00 (only achieved in pessimistic scenario), ≈ –13–15%. California already at structural –6% YoY at $5.85 baseline.
- Key non-linearity: at sustained $5.50+ national for 3+ months, the income channel (real wage erosion) compounds the substitution channel and you get >2x the static elasticity. This is what the IEA April OMR is implicitly modeling in its –240 kb/d OECD demand revision.

### "Rockets and Feathers" Asymmetry
The Bacon (1991) asymmetric pass-through model, validated repeatedly (FTC 2011; ECB 2024), implies that on the way up (May 2026), retail gasoline rises ~85% as fast as wholesale; on the way down (Q3-Q4), retail falls only ~55% as fast. This adds ~$0.18/gal "stickiness" to our Q4 2026 retail forecasts vs. a symmetric model. We have built this into the path. This is the main reason national retail does not return to ~$3.10/gal even as Brent settles at $80.

### War-Risk Insurance & Freight
- Per-voyage Hormuz war risk premium 2.5–5% of hull value = $6–10M above January baseline. This is the binding non-flow constraint even after a ceasefire.
- Forecast: insurance premiums fall 50% by August (base) on multilateral escort regime; another 50% by Q1 2027.
- VLCC clean tanker rates +200% YoY through summer; LR2 rates +180%. Freight cost pass-through to refined product prices in Asia is the key reason Brent–WTI spread persists at $13–15/bbl through Q3 (vs. $6 normal).

### Saudi East-West Pipeline (Petroline)
Confirmed at 7.0 mb/d capacity (incl. NGL pipe converted to crude) since March 11. April 9 Iranian drone attack reduced 700 kbpd briefly; restored April 12. Yanbu crude exports ≈5 mb/d; refined products 700–900 kbpd. **This is the single most important asset offsetting Hormuz. If a future strike disables Petroline (the model gives ~12% probability over 6 months), Brent jumps $20–30/bbl in days.**

### Qatar LNG Structural Hit
- Trains 4 & 6 destroyed = 12.8 mtpa = 17% of Qatar exports = ~3% of global LNG.
- Repair: 3–5 years; turbine lead time 2–4 years (only 3 manufacturers worldwide).
- Force majeure to China, South Korea, Italy, Belgium for up to 5 years.
- Wood Mackenzie: Ras Laffan won't be fully online before end-August even on remaining trains.
- **Persistent TTF premium**: €15–25/MWh above pre-war baseline through 2027 in base case. Drives sustained Atlantic LNG arbitrage and Henry Hub support — even with US gas inventories above 5-yr average.

## Caveats and Uncertainty

1. **All event probabilities reflect my judgment given the unprecedented combination of: a kinetic naval engagement (Spruance/Touska April 19), a still-active mine threat (additional mines week of April 23), a rejected Iranian proposal (April 28), and a Pentagon 6-month mine-clearance timeline.** None of the major bank scenarios (Goldman, JPM, Citi, Morgan Stanley, BofA) has all four of these factors weighted simultaneously.
2. **The 45% pessimistic weight is higher than the consensus** because the geometric distribution of incidents over an extended blockade implies ~3–5% per week of escalation; over 8 weeks (May–June) that compounds to ~30–35% even before considering Iran's incentive structure post-blockade.
3. **JPMorgan's "operational minimum" warning for May 9–30** is the single most important date window. This is where Asian commercial inventories (China, India, Japan, South Korea) hit hard storage floors. Either Hormuz materially reopens by then, or the curve repositions to $130–150 within a 2-week window. The futures market is currently pricing ~25% probability of this.
4. **EIA April STEO is invalidated for the post-April outlook** because it explicitly assumed "the conflict does not persist past April." Its $115 Q2 / $99.80 Q3 / $88 Q4 path was built on shut-ins falling from 9.1 mb/d (April) to 6.7 (May) to near-zero late-2026. We instead use the IEA April OMR's framework, which models persistent disruption.
5. **The OPEC MOMR demand number (106.5 mb/d) is anchoring on growth optimism that is now physically impossible** at $100+ Brent and Asian fuel rationing. Treat it as a ceiling, not a center.
6. **California prices are the most uncertain regional forecast.** USC Marshall (Mische) modeled a worst-case $7.35–$8.44/gal by year-end 2026 even before the war; UC Davis modeled $1.21/gal additive. Stack a war + Q3 unplanned outage + LCFS update + summer blend = realistic stress case $7.50+. The state's geographic isolation, blend specificity, and absence of pipeline connectivity to PADD 3 mean any single-refinery problem propagates a $0.50+/gal price spike in days.
7. **Fed policy reaction function is uncertain in stagflationary conditions.** History (1973–75, 1979–81) suggests central banks initially look-through energy supply shocks but pivot when long-run inflation expectations un-anchor. UMich 5–10 yr expectations at 3.5% (highest since October 2025) is an early warning. If they reach 4.0% before September, the first cut is delayed indefinitely.
8. **The fertilizer-to-food-CPI channel is the most under-modeled in mainstream forecasts.** Most CPI forecasts (EY, Goldman) assume food-at-home stays near 4% YoY through 2026, missing the Q4 inflection from under-fertilized 2026 crops. The Q1 2027 grocery-bill print could materially exceed expectations and dominate the political narrative.
9. **Base case forecasts after September 2026 are progressively less reliable** as cumulative scenario divergence widens; treat anything beyond Q4 2026 as a directional rather than precise call. The 90% confidence interval for Brent in Q1 2027 in this model is $66–$112.
10. **Source-quality flag**: A meaningful share of the most current (April 2026) data points were drawn from secondary aggregators (TradingEconomics, OilPrice) when primary EIA/IEA data was not yet released for April 28. Numbers consistent with two or more independent sources have been preferred. Where bank scenario matrices (Citi 50/30/20, Goldman base/severe, JPM mid-May trigger) conflicted, I used the median of the published forecast ladders. The exact close on April 28 of $111.26 Brent / $99.93 WTI as stated in the brief is consistent with TradingEconomics, Trading View, and Investing.com close data within a $1 range.
11. **Structural shifts** — UAE OPEC exit (announced end of April 28 week), Qatar LNG long-tail damage, US-China teapot refinery sanctions, EU Russian LNG ban April 25 — collectively move the post-war steady-state price floor up by ~$8–12/bbl vs. pre-war assumptions, even after Hormuz fully normalizes. This is why this model's Q1 2027 base case ($80) exceeds EIA's pre-war 2027 forecast ($53) by ~50% and exceeds the December 2026 WTI futures price ($79.66) only modestly. The market is currently pricing a faster normalization than I forecast; my base case is mildly bearish vs. the futures curve in 2027 because I expect the structural premium to fade as new LNG supply, US shale recovery, and Iranian return-to-market materialize through 2027.
