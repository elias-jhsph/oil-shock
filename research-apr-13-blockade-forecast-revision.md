# Five days that rewrote the oil forecast

**The ceasefire-to-blockade reversal between April 8–13 has fundamentally altered every scenario in the original April 8 forecast.** What began as a historic risk-premium unwind — WTI plunging 16.4% in a single session — ended with Trump ordering a US naval blockade of Iranian ports after the Islamabad peace talks collapsed on April 12. The base case must now shift from "gradual Hormuz normalization" to "prolonged restricted access with active naval confrontation." Pessimistic scenario probability should increase from 30% to at least 45%, while optimistic drops from 20% to 10%. The original report's assumption that ceasefire → diplomatic resolution → phased Hormuz reopening was invalidated within 120 hours.

The five-day window produced a cascade of first-order data releases — March CPI (+0.9% m/m, the largest gasoline-driven spike since 1967), record-low consumer sentiment (47.6), the EIA's April STEO projecting **9.1 million b/d of Gulf shut-ins**, and IEA Director Birol's April 13 estimate of **13 million b/d shuttered** — that collectively confirm the original report's pessimistic macro scenario is now materializing faster than projected. Moody's recession probability sits near **49%**, and Goldman has pushed its first Fed rate cut expectation from June to September.

## The Islamabad collapse and naval blockade change everything

The highest-level direct US-Iran engagement since 1979 began at Islamabad's Serena Hotel on April 11, with VP Vance leading a 300-member US delegation against Iran's 70-member team led by Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf and FM Araghchi. After **21 continuous hours of negotiation**, talks collapsed on April 12 over four non-negotiable US demands: end all uranium enrichment, remove highly enriched uranium stockpiles, cease funding of Hamas/Hezbollah/Houthis, and fully open Hormuz without tolls. Iran rejected all four.

Trump's post-collapse response was swift and escalatory. He announced a **US Navy blockade of all Iranian ports** beginning 10 a.m. ET Monday, April 13, targeting all ships entering or exiting Iranian ports on the Arabian Gulf and Gulf of Oman, plus interdiction of any vessel that paid Iran's $2M toll. CENTCOM clarified the blockade would not impede non-Iranian port traffic — but the operational distinction is narrow in a waterway where vessels transit within nautical miles of Iranian territorial waters. The first test came immediately: the Comoros-registered tanker **Elpis** (a sanctioned shadow-fleet vessel) defied the blockade and passed through, while the Botswana-registered **Ostria** turned back 41 minutes after the deadline.

This development is the single most consequential update to the original forecast. The ceasefire technically holds — no direct US-Iran kinetic strikes have resumed — but a naval blockade is functionally an act of war under international law. Iran's acting defense minister declared armed forces on "maximum combat alert." France's Macron announced plans for a "peaceful multinational mission" to restore freedom of navigation. The remaining 5 days of the two-week ceasefire window now carry significantly elevated escalation risk.

**For the forecast: every timeline assumption predicated on "ceasefire holds → talks succeed → phased reopening" requires revision. The blockade introduces a new variable — potential naval confrontation — that did not exist in the April 8 scenario framework.**

The blockade has re-established $100+ as the operating range. Week-by-week price forecasts for April 14–30 should be revised upward by $8–15/bbl across all scenarios.**

## The Strait of Hormuz remains functionally closed

Despite the ceasefire, the Strait of Hormuz has not meaningfully reopened. Between the ceasefire announcement and April 11, only **22 vessels exited with AIS transponders on** (S&P Global), versus a pre-war average of **~135 per day**. Critically, Kpler reported that only **2 oil or gas tankers** transited in the first two days post-ceasefire. Traffic peaked at 17 transits on April 11, then collapsed again as talks failed.

The physical barriers remain formidable:

- **Mines**: Iran planted at least a dozen underwater mines including **Maham 3** (moored with sensors) and **Maham 7** (bottom-resting). Per the NYT, **Iran cannot locate all mines it planted** and lacks capacity to remove them. Trump claimed all Iranian mine-laying ships were sunk — true but irrelevant to removal.
- **Mine clearance**: Two US Navy destroyers (USS Frank E. Peterson, USS Michael Murphy) entered the Strait on April 11 — the first US warships since the war began February 28. CENTCOM announced "setting conditions" for clearance with underwater drones. But these are NOT specialized mine-countermeasure vessels; the Navy retired its last Avenger-class MCM ship from Bahrain last year, replacing it with LCS platforms that have limited testing.
- **Iran's toll regime**: The $2M per-vessel toll is active, codified by Iran's parliament as the "Strait of Hormuz Management Plan." Payments accepted in Chinese yuan via CIPS/Kunlun Bank and **Bitcoin/USDT** — the first state use of cryptocurrency at a major maritime chokepoint. Iran operates a five-tier nationality system: "friendly" nations (China, Russia, Pakistan) get lower rates; US/Israel-linked vessels denied entirely. The IMO declared the tolls illegal under UNCLOS; Trump ordered interdiction of any vessel that paid.
- **Stranded fleet**: **600+ vessels** including 325 tankers remain stuck in or near the Gulf (Lloyd's List). The IMO Secretary-General estimates ~2,000 ships total waiting on both sides.

**ADNOC CEO Sultan Al Jaber stated bluntly on April 9: "The Strait of Hormuz is not open. Access is being restricted, conditioned and controlled."** Iran was limiting passage to a maximum of 15 vessels per day, routed through an altered northern corridor near Larak Island rather than standard shipping lanes, and maintained an IRGC escort requirement.

**For the forecast: the original report's Hormuz restoration timeline — presumably assuming initial vessel transits within days, insurance normalization within weeks, and full traffic restoration within 2–3 months — must be extended by at least 4–8 weeks. The naval blockade creates a new timeline uncertainty: even if the ceasefire holds, the blockade effectively prevents normalization of non-Chinese/Russian traffic to Iranian ports, and mine-clearing cannot proceed safely under current conditions.**

## Supply-demand balance tightening beyond April 8 estimates

Three major institutional reports provide updated supply-demand framing:

**EIA STEO (released April 7)**: Projects Gulf shut-ins rising from 7.5M b/d in March to **9.1M b/d in April** (peak), declining to 6.7M b/d in May, and returning near pre-conflict levels by late 2026. Brent forecast: peak **$115/b in Q2**, declining to $88/b by Q4. Global demand growth slashed to **0.6M b/d** (from 1.2M b/d pre-conflict). US gasoline forecast to peak at ~$4.30/gal monthly average in April; diesel above $5.80/gal.

**IEA (Birol, April 13 Atlantic Council)**: Characterizes the crisis as **~13 million b/d shuttered** — larger than 1973 and 1979 combined. Called it "the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market." Stated oil prices "don't yet reflect the severity" of the crisis, with **40+ energy assets across 9 countries "severely damaged."**

**OPEC MOMR (April)**: Maintained full-year 2026 demand growth at 1.4M b/d but cut Q2 demand by **500,000 bpd** citing "transitory economic weakness." OPEC+ met April 5; next meeting May 3.

The **EIA Weekly Petroleum Status Report** (released April 8, data for week ending April 3) showed US crude inventories up **3.08M barrels** to **464.7M barrels** — well above expectations, driven by SPR releases flowing into commercial stocks plus reduced exports. Distillate stocks fell 3.14M barrels to 114.7M, sitting **5.66M barrels below** the 5-year average — a tightness signal for diesel/heating oil. Refinery utilization held at 92.0%.

The **SPR drawdown** continues on schedule. The DOE released a second tranche of **8.48 million barrels** on April 12 to Gunvor, Phillips 66, Trafigura, and Macquarie. The IEA's coordinated 400M barrel release across 32 countries is history's largest. Post-release, the US SPR will fall to approximately **243 million barrels** — the lowest level since 1982.

**Baker Hughes rig count** (April 10): US total rigs at **545** (−3 week-over-week, −38 year-over-year). Oil rigs flat at 411; gas rigs down 3. No meaningful shale response yet to elevated prices — consistent with producers' stated capital discipline.

**For the forecast: the EIA's April STEO, completed April 6, assumed conflict does not persist past April and Hormuz gradually resumes. Both assumptions are now questionable. The 9.1M b/d April shut-in figure likely underestimates reality given IEA's 13M b/d estimate. The May figure (6.7M b/d) should be revised upward to 8–9M b/d given the blockade and failed talks. SPR releases are providing a meaningful cushion but the drawdown trajectory — to 1982 levels — limits future flexibility.**

## Infrastructure damage: facility-by-facility update

The conflict produced extensive damage across the Gulf. Updated facility assessments based on April 8–13 reporting:

**Iran:** Kharg Island oil infrastructure remains **intact and operational** — the US deliberately avoided striking oil facilities there, targeting only military installations. TankerTrackers confirmed tankers still loading post-strikes. However, **Bandar Abbas** port suffered extensive damage (40+ facilities per Oregon State SAR analysis), and South Pars/Asaluyeh was struck by Israel, triggering the retaliatory attack on Qatar's Ras Laffan.

**Saudi Arabia (SPA report, April 9):** Attacks cut Saudi production capacity by **~600,000 bpd** and East-West Pipeline throughput by **~700,000 bpd**. Facilities hit include Ras Tanura refinery (550K bpd, hit twice), SATORP Jubail, SAMREF Yanbu, Riyadh refinery, Ju'aymah LPG processing, Manifa/Khurais fields, and a pipeline pumping station. Shaybah (1M bpd capacity) was repeatedly targeted but reportedly undamaged.

**Qatar:** Ras Laffan LNG trains 4 and 6 are **destroyed** — removing 12.8 mtpa or ~17% of Qatar's LNG exports for **3–5 years** due to 2–4 year turbine delivery lead times. QatarEnergy declared force majeure on long-term contracts with China, South Korea, Italy, and Belgium for up to 5 years. Revenue loss estimated at **~$20 billion annually**. The North site (41 mtpa) could restart within ~1 month; the directly-hit South site not before end of summer at earliest.

**UAE:** ADNOC Ruwais refinery (922K bpd, world's 4th largest single-site) shut since the March 10 drone strike, with additional fires on April 5. Recovery timeline: **6–12 months** for full capacity. Shah gas field suspended after March 16 drone attack. Das Island LNG operating at minimal levels.

**Iraq:** Basra Oil Company pumping **~900,000 bpd** (down from 3.2M pre-war, a 72% decline). Rumaila at 400K bpd (from 1.35M); Zubair at 300K bpd (from 640K). Cause is export bottleneck, not infrastructure damage — storage filled up with Hormuz closed. BOC claims it could reach 2M bpd "within hours" and 3M bpd "within days" once exports resume, but independent analysts estimate **2–6 months** for full recovery to 4M+ bpd. Iraq approved a $4.6 billion alternative Basra-Haditha pipeline project on April 9. Only ~250K bpd currently exports via the Kirkuk-Ceyhan pipeline to Turkey.

**Other Gulf states:** BAPCO Bahrain declared force majeure after refinery attacks. Kuwait's Mina Al-Ahmadi (730K bpd) hit by drone attack April 3, with KPC declaring force majeure. An estimated **136 million barrels** of crude/products are physically stuck in the Gulf (Macquarie).

**For the forecast: the original damage assessment likely underestimated Qatar LNG damage severity (3–5 year timeline is far longer than most initial estimates). Saudi capacity loss (~1.3M bpd combined production + pipeline) is confirmed. The Iraq recovery timeline should use the conservative 2–6 month estimate rather than BOC's optimistic "hours to days" claim, given foreign company departures and operational disruptions. Total permanently lost capacity may reach 1–2M bpd (ANZ estimate).**

## The macro cascade is accelerating

**March CPI (released April 10):** Headline inflation surged **+0.9% month-over-month** — the highest monthly increase since June 2022 — driven almost entirely by a **+21.2% monthly jump in gasoline prices**, the largest on record since the BLS began tracking in 1967. Year-over-year headline reached **3.3%** (from 2.4% in February). However, **core CPI came in below expectations** at +0.2% m/m and 2.6% y/y, providing the Fed some cover to "look through" the energy spike. Shelter continued moderating at +3.0% y/y (tied lowest since August 2021). Goldman Sachs Asset Management noted: "We believe the Fed will look through the energy-driven noise so long as these factors hold."

**Consumer sentiment (U Michigan preliminary, April 10):** Crashed to **47.6 — an all-time record low**, below the 1980 energy crisis reading of 51.7 and the pandemic trough. One-year inflation expectations surged to **4.8%** from 3.8%, the largest one-month jump since April 2025. Critically, **98% of surveys were conducted before the ceasefire announcement**, meaning next month's reading could improve — or not, given the blockade.

**Fed positioning:** Chair Powell (Harvard, March 30) said the 3.5%–3.75% rate is "a good place" and sees no need to hike due to the oil shock. SF Fed's Daly laid out three scenarios with declining probability: quick resolution (look through), elevated persistence (hold steady), and rate hike (lowest probability). Governor Barr struck a more hawkish tone, warning that "yet another price shock could increase longer-term inflation expectations" after five years of elevated inflation. **No emergency meetings have been announced.** Market pricing shows little chance of cuts or hikes through year-end.

**Airlines are bleeding:** Delta reported Q1 net loss of $289M (vs. $240M profit year-ago), with jet fuel adding **~$400M in costs in March alone**. Targeting flat year-over-year capacity for Q2. United cut **5% of planned capacity** — the first major carrier to act — with CEO Kirby modeling oil at $175/bbl for stress tests. American Airlines' annual fuel bill could swell by **$11 billion**; UBS slashed its 2026 EPS estimate from >$2.00 to $0.43. All major carriers raised baggage fees.

**Fertilizer crisis compounds food inflation risk:** Urea surged to **$604–$745/ton** from ~$400 at the start of the year (up ~50-85%). Approximately **30% of global urea trade** originates from Hormuz-constrained countries, and QatarEnergy halted downstream urea production entirely. The Fertilizer Institute predicts US farmers will be **short ~2 million tons of urea** this spring planting season. The IMF warned 45 million additional people face hunger risk globally. This second-round effect will drive food-price inflation in H2 2026, creating a wave the Fed cannot easily "look through."

**Recession probabilities** have clustered in the 30–49% range: Moody's Analytics at **~49%** (Zandi: "if oil prices remain elevated for much longer, a recession will be difficult to avoid"), JPMorgan at **35%**, Goldman at **30%**, EY-Parthenon at **40%**. Four of five major oil shocks since 1970 have been followed by recession. The key threshold per Moody's modeling: sustained oil above $125/bbl tips the economy.

## Revised scenario probabilities and key monitoring triggers

The original April 8 report's probability weights (20% optimistic / 50% base / 30% pessimistic) must be substantially revised based on five developments: talks failure, naval blockade, mine-clearing complications, toll regime entrenchment, and the insurance market's refusal to normalize.

**Recommended revised weights:**

- **Optimistic (10%, down from 20%):** Requires back-channel diplomacy to produce a breakthrough within the remaining ceasefire window (expires ~April 21), blockade lifted, Iran drops toll and enrichment demands, mine-clearing accelerated with international cooperation. Brent returns to $80–90 by May, $70–75 by Q3. *Trigger to monitor: any resumption of direct talks, Pakistan-mediated compromise, or Trump "art of the deal" surprise.*

- **Base case (45%, down from 50%):** Ceasefire technically holds but blockade creates a new equilibrium of restricted Hormuz access. Chinese/Russian vessels continue transiting; Western/allied shipping stays away. Oil stabilizes in the **$95–$115 range** through Q2, gradually declining as alternative routes, SPR releases, and demand destruction take effect. Hormuz does not return to pre-war traffic levels until Q4 2026 at earliest. Fed holds rates. Recession narrowly avoided but GDP growth near zero in Q3. *This scenario requires the blockade to remain non-kinetic and both sides to exercise restraint.*

- **Pessimistic (45%, up from 30%):** Naval confrontation incident (accidental or intentional) collapses the ceasefire. Active hostilities resume. Houthis open a "second chokepoint" at Bab al-Mandeb. Oil spikes above $130, with Dated Brent potentially revisiting $150+. Recession becomes inevitable. Fed faces impossible trilemma of inflation/recession/financial stability. SPR drawdown accelerates to critically low levels. Food crisis materializes in H2 2026. *Trigger: any kinetic exchange between US and Iranian naval forces, Houthi escalation, or Israel expanding operations against Iran directly.*

**Key data releases to watch in the next 7 days:**

- **April 14**: PPI (March), EIA Monthly Oil Statistics, EIA Gasoline & Diesel Update (PADD-level for week of April 7)
- **April 15**: EIA Weekly Petroleum Status Report (week ending April 10 — first full post-ceasefire inventory data)
- **April 16–17**: Industrial production, housing starts
- **April 18**: Baker Hughes rig count
- **April 21**: Retail sales (March, rescheduled)
- **~April 21**: Two-week ceasefire expiration date

The ceasefire expiration around April 21 is the single highest-consequence date on the calendar. Without extension or a new diplomatic framework, the legal basis for the current quasi-stable equilibrium vanishes — and the naval blockade, mine-clearing operations, and Iranian military posture would all face a decision point simultaneously.

**Bottom line:** The original April 8 forecast was built on a foundation of cautious optimism — ceasefire announced, talks scheduled, prices crashing. Five days later, that foundation has cracked. The Islamabad collapse, the blockade, and the persistent physical closure of Hormuz have collectively pushed the forecast toward its pessimistic scenario faster than any reasonable model would have anticipated. Every price deck in the original report needs to be revised upward by $8–15/bbl for April–May, and recovery timelines extended by 4–8 weeks at minimum. The secondary cascade — CPI, fertilizer, airlines, consumer sentiment — is no longer a tail risk but an active, measurable economic shock. The next 8 days until ceasefire expiration will determine whether this becomes a managed crisis or a generational economic event.
